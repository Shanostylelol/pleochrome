## 1. Reg D 506(c) -- Complete Process

### 1.1 What It Is

Rule 506(c) of Regulation D is an exemption from Securities Act registration that allows issuers to raise an **unlimited amount of capital** from **accredited investors only**, with the critical advantage of permitting **general solicitation and advertising**. This was created by the JOBS Act (2012) and implemented by the SEC in September 2013.

Key characteristics:
- **No cap** on the total amount raised
- **No limit** on the number of accredited investors
- **General solicitation permitted** (ads, social media, public marketing)
- **No SEC review or qualification** period (unlike Reg A+)
- Issuer must take **"reasonable steps to verify"** accredited investor status
- Securities issued are **"restricted securities"** (cannot be freely resold)

### 1.2 Why 506(c) Is the Right Exemption for PleoChrome

For a $10-18M tokenized gemstone offering, 506(c) is the optimal choice because:

| Factor | 506(c) | 506(b) | Reg A+ | Reg CF |
|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| Max raise | Unlimited | Unlimited | $75M | $5M |
| General solicitation | YES | No | Yes | Yes |
| Non-accredited investors | No | Up to 35 | Yes | Yes |
| SEC qualification needed | No | No | Yes (months) | Yes |
| Ongoing reporting | Minimal | Minimal | Extensive | Extensive |
| Timeline to first sale | 4-8 weeks | 4-8 weeks | 6-12 months | 2-4 months |
| Investor verification | Required | Self-cert OK | N/A | N/A |
| Cost to launch | $40K-$100K | $25K-$60K | $150K-$500K+ | $50K-$150K |

**Bottom line:** 506(c) lets PleoChrome publicly market the offering (essential for attracting high-net-worth gemstone investors), has no dollar cap, requires no SEC qualification delay, and costs dramatically less than Reg A+. The tradeoff -- accredited investors only with verification -- is acceptable because the $10-18M price point naturally targets institutional and HNW investors anyway.

### 1.3 Filing Requirements

**Federal (SEC):**
- File **Form D** via EDGAR within **15 days** after the first sale of securities
- No SEC review or approval required -- it is a notice filing only
- No filing fee

**State (Blue Sky):**
- File notice filings in each state where securities are **sold** (not offered)
- Most states require a copy of Form D + filing fee ($50-$750, varies by state)
- Must file within **15 days** of first sale in that state
- Typically 46 states require notice filing

**Critical note:** Unlike Reg A+ which requires SEC qualification (6-12 months), 506(c) has **no waiting period**. You can sell as soon as documents are ready and an investor is verified.

### 1.5 Accredited Investor Requirements

Under 506(c), **every single purchaser** must be an accredited investor. There are no exceptions, no sophistication alternatives, and no numerical limits.

**Natural persons qualify if they have:**
- Income exceeding **$200,000** (or $300,000 joint with spouse) in each of the two most recent years with a reasonable expectation of the same in the current year, OR
- Net worth exceeding **$1,000,000** (individually or jointly), excluding the value of primary residence, OR
- Hold certain professional certifications (Series 7, 65, or 82 licenses), OR
- Are "knowledgeable employees" of a private fund

**Entities qualify if they have:**
- Total assets exceeding **$5,000,000**, OR
- All equity owners are individually accredited investors, OR
- Are a bank, insurance company, registered investment company, or other specified institutional investor

### 1.6 Verification Methods

The SEC's March 12, 2025 no-action letter significantly simplified verification. Three approaches:

**A. Minimum Investment Threshold (NEW - March 2025)**
- If the minimum investment is **$200,000+** for natural persons or **$1,000,000+** for entities
- The issuer obtains **written self-certification** that the investor is accredited and the investment is not third-party financed
- No additional verification steps required
- **This is a game-changer for PleoChrome** given the high-value nature of the offering

**B. Traditional Verification Methods (still valid)**
- **Income:** Review tax returns (W-2, 1099, K-1, etc.) for the two most recent years plus a written representation of expected income in the current year
- **Net worth:** Review bank statements, brokerage statements, appraisal reports, and obtain a written representation that all liabilities have been disclosed
- **Professional certification:** Verify the investor holds a Series 7, 65, or 82 license

**C. Third-Party Verification**
- Obtain a written confirmation from a registered broker-dealer, SEC-registered investment adviser, licensed attorney, or CPA that they have verified the investor's accredited status within the prior three months

### 1.7 General Solicitation Rules

**What You CAN Do Under 506(c):**
- Advertise the offering on social media (LinkedIn, Twitter/X, Instagram, etc.)
- Post offering details on your company website
- Run digital advertising campaigns (Google Ads, programmatic ads, etc.)
- Use traditional media (TV, radio, print)
- Host public events, webinars, and seminars
- Use email marketing campaigns to broad audiences
- List on investment platforms and crowdfunding portals
- Issue press releases about the offering
- Use broker-dealer distribution networks

**What You CANNOT Do:**
- Make false or misleading statements (anti-fraud rules always apply)
- Omit material information that makes other statements misleading
- Use projections or forward-looking statements without a reasonable basis
- Guarantee returns or promise specific outcomes
- Sell to anyone who is not a verified accredited investor
- Fail to include appropriate disclaimers and risk disclosures
- Make claims about SEC approval or endorsement (the SEC never "approves" offerings)
- Mislead investors about the nature of restricted securities or transfer limitations

**Practical guidance for PleoChrome marketing:**
- All marketing materials should be reviewed by securities counsel before publication
- Include standard disclaimers: "This is not an offer to sell or solicitation to buy. Securities are offered only to verified accredited investors via the Private Placement Memorandum."
- Keep records of all advertising materials used in the offering
- Apply the same rigor to advertising as you would to any document subject to securities law

### 1.8 Ongoing Reporting Obligations

Unlike Reg A+ or public companies, 506(c) issuers have **minimal ongoing reporting obligations**:
- **Annual Form D amendment** if the offering continues beyond 12 months
- **Blue Sky renewals** in applicable states
- **Investor communications** as specified in the operating/partnership agreement (typically quarterly or annual financial updates)
- No requirement to file with the SEC on an ongoing basis (no 10-K, 10-Q, 8-K)
- Must maintain books and records related to the offering for at least **5 years**

## 3. The PPM (Private Placement Memorandum)

### 3.1 What the PPM Must Contain

The PPM is the primary disclosure document provided to prospective investors. While not technically "required" for 506(c) offerings (unlike 506(b) with non-accredited investors), **in practice every serious offering includes one**. It is the issuer's primary legal protection against fraud claims and establishes that investors were given all material information.

#### Required/Expected Sections:

**1. Cover Page**
- Offering name and issuer
- Type of securities offered
- Total offering amount
- Minimum investment amount
- Date of the PPM
- Confidentiality legend
- Disclaimer: "These securities have not been registered under the Securities Act of 1933..."
- Statement that the SEC has not approved or disapproved the securities

**2. Table of Contents**

**3. Summary of the Offering**
- Brief description of the issuer and its business
- Securities being offered (token structure, rights, limitations)
- Total offering amount and minimum/maximum raise
- Minimum investment per investor
- Use of proceeds (high-level)
- Investor eligibility requirements
- Key dates and milestones

**4. Risk Factors**
The most critical section. Must be comprehensive, specific, and prioritized. For a tokenized gemstone offering, risks should include:
- **Gemstone-specific risks:** Valuation uncertainty, market volatility, illiquidity of physical gemstones, storage/custody risk, insurance coverage gaps, GIA grading disputes
- **Tokenization risks:** Smart contract vulnerabilities, blockchain network risks, technology obsolescence, regulatory uncertainty around digital securities
- **Securities risks:** Restricted securities with no public market, limited transferability, potential loss of entire investment, no guaranteed returns
- **Business risks:** Key person dependency, early-stage venture, unproven business model, competition
- **Regulatory risks:** Changing SEC/CFTC guidance, state regulatory actions, potential reclassification
- **Custody risks:** Third-party custodian default, insurance limits, force majeure
- **Market risks:** Gemstone price fluctuations, economic downturn, reduced demand for alternative assets
- **Tax risks:** Uncertain tax treatment of tokenized assets, potential collectibles tax rate (28% for gemstones)
- **Liquidity risks:** No secondary market guarantee, holding period restrictions

**5. Description of the Business**
- Company history and formation
- Business model and revenue sources
- The gemstone barrel -- detailed description, provenance, grading
- Custody and insurance arrangements
- GIA certification details
- Appraisal methodology and independent valuation
- Competitive landscape

**6. Management Team**
- Biographical information on all officers, directors, managers, and key persons
- Relevant experience and qualifications
- Compensation arrangements
- Conflicts of interest
- Bad actor disclosure (Rule 506(d) compliance)

**7. Terms of the Offering**
- Description of the tokens/securities (economic rights, voting rights, distribution rights)
- Price per token and total tokens offered
- Minimum and maximum offering amounts
- Subscription process and closing conditions
- Escrow arrangements (if any)
- Conditions to closing

**8. Use of Proceeds**
- Detailed breakdown of how funds will be used
- Table showing allocation percentages:
  - Gemstone acquisition/custody costs
  - Insurance and storage
  - Legal and compliance
  - Technology (tokenization platform, smart contracts)
  - Marketing and distribution
  - Working capital
  - Management fees
  - Reserves

**9. Description of the Securities**
- Token structure and standard (ERC-20, ERC-1400, etc.)
- Rights and privileges of token holders
- Transfer restrictions and lock-up periods
- Redemption rights (if any)
- Distribution/dividend policy
- Voting rights (if any)
- Anti-dilution protections

**10. Plan of Distribution**
- How securities will be marketed and sold
- Broker-dealer involvement (if any)
- Commission structure
- General solicitation strategy

**11. Investor Suitability Standards**
- Accredited investor requirements
- Verification procedures
- Minimum investment amount
- Suitability representations required

**12. Tax Considerations**
- Federal income tax treatment
- State tax implications
- Collectibles tax treatment (28% rate for gemstones held >1 year)
- UBTI considerations for tax-exempt investors
- Foreign investor withholding
- Disclaimers to consult own tax adviser

**13. ERISA Considerations**
- Whether the investment qualifies for ERISA plans
- "Plan asset" rules
- Fiduciary responsibility considerations

**14. Legal Matters**
- Litigation disclosure
- Regulatory proceedings
- Legal counsel opinion

**15. Conflicts of Interest**
- Related party transactions
- Management compensation
- Dual roles
- Affiliate transactions

**16. Financial Statements**
- Audited financials (if available)
- Pro forma projections (with reasonable basis)
- Balance sheet
- Income/expense projections

**17. Exhibits**
- Subscription Agreement
- Operating Agreement / LP Agreement
- Investor Questionnaire
- Accredited Investor Certification Form

### 3.2 How the PPM Is Structured

The standard PPM document package includes **6-10 interlocking documents**:

1. **PPM itself** (the disclosure document) -- 60-150 pages
2. **Subscription Agreement** -- investor's binding commitment to purchase
3. **Operating Agreement** (LLC) or **Limited Partnership Agreement** -- governance document
4. **Investor Questionnaire** -- suitability and accreditation questions
5. **Accredited Investor Certification** -- verification form
6. **Escrow Agreement** (if applicable) -- third-party escrow for investor funds
7. **Token Purchase Agreement** -- specific to tokenized offerings
8. **Smart Contract Audit Report** -- for tokenized securities

| Component | Cost | Timeline |
|-----------|------|----------|
| PPM drafting (attorney) | $25,000 - $75,000 | 3-6 weeks |
| Subscription agreement | Included or $3,000 - $8,000 | 1-2 weeks |
| Operating agreement | Included or $5,000 - $15,000 | 2-3 weeks |

**For PleoChrome's tokenized gemstone offering:** Expect the higher end of these ranges due to:

- GIA certification and appraisal methodology must be thoroughly documented

### 3.4 Who Reviews/Approves the PPM

- **Securities counsel** drafts and has primary responsibility
- **Tax counsel** reviews tax sections (may be same firm or separate)
- **Management/issuer** reviews for factual accuracy
- **Broker-dealer** (if involved) files with FINRA within 15 calendar days of first sale
- **The SEC does NOT review or approve PPMs** for Reg D offerings
- **State regulators** generally do not review PPMs for Rule 506 offerings (federal preemption)

### 3.5 Common PPM Mistakes That Create Legal Liability

1. **Insufficient risk factors** -- failing to disclose material risks is the #1 source of liability
2. **Overly optimistic projections** -- projections without reasonable basis invite fraud claims
3. **Stale financial information** -- using outdated financials
4. **Missing conflicts of interest** -- failing to disclose related-party transactions
5. **Inadequate management bios** -- omitting relevant negative history
6. **Boilerplate risk factors** -- generic risks without offering-specific details
7. **Inconsistency between PPM and marketing materials** -- advertising claims that exceed PPM disclosures
8. **Missing bad actor disclosure** -- failing to disclose covered persons' disqualifying events
9. **Inadequate use of proceeds** -- vague descriptions of how funds will be used
10. **Not updating** -- failing to amend when material facts change

## 4. Form D Filing

### 4.1 Step-by-Step EDGAR Filing Process

**Step 1: Create Login.gov Account**
- Visit login.gov and create individual account credentials
- Required for all EDGAR access since September 15, 2025 ("EDGAR Next" system)
- Must set up multifactor authentication

**Step 2: Obtain EDGAR CIK Number**
- Submit **Form ID** to request EDGAR access
- Requires identifying information about the issuer
- Notarized authentication document required
- EDGAR will email the CIK (Central Index Key) number to account administrators
- Processing time: typically 1-2 business days

**Step 3: Log Into EDGAR Online Forms**
- Visit SEC's Online Forms Login page
- Log in using CIK number and EDGAR access codes
- Choose "Form D" under "Make a Filing"
- **Important: You have only ONE HOUR** after your last keystroke to complete the filing

**Step 4: Complete the Form D**
- Can compile information using a paper version first before logging in
- Recommend having all information ready before starting the online filing

### 4.2 Information Required on Form D

Form D is relatively brief (compared to other SEC filings) but requires:

**Item 1: Issuer's Identity**
- Legal name of issuer
- State/country of incorporation or organization
- CIK number
- IRS Employer Identification Number (EIN)
- Year and state of incorporation
- Issuer type (corporation, LLC, LP, etc.)

**Item 2: Principal Place of Business**
- Street address, city, state, zip code
- Phone number

**Item 3: Related Persons**
- Names and addresses of executive officers, directors, and promoters
- Relationship to issuer

**Item 4: Industry Group**
- Classification of issuer's business

**Item 5: Issuer Size**
- Revenue range or aggregate net asset value (for funds)

**Item 6: Federal Exemption(s) and Exclusion(s) Claimed**
- Check "Rule 506(c)" specifically
- Can claim multiple exemptions (e.g., Rule 506(c) + Regulation S for international investors)

**Item 7: Type of Filing**
- New Notice or Amendment
- Date of first sale (or "yet to occur")

**Item 8: Duration of Offering**
- Whether the offering is still ongoing or has been completed

**Item 9: Type(s) of Securities Offered**
- Equity, debt, or other (specify -- for tokens, likely "Other" with description)

**Item 10: Business Combination Transaction**
- Whether the offering involves a business combination

**Item 11: Minimum Investment Accepted from Any Outside Investor**
- Dollar amount

**Item 12: Sales Compensation**
- Name and address of each person compensated for soliciting investors
- Including broker-dealers, finders, and their CRD numbers

**Item 13: Offering and Sales Amounts**
- Total offering amount (or "indefinite" if no maximum)
- Total amount sold to date
- Total remaining to be sold
- Whether sales have already occurred

**Item 14: Investors**
- Number of accredited investors who have already invested
- Number of non-accredited investors (should be ZERO for 506(c))
- Total number of investors

**Item 15: Sales Commissions and Finder's Fees Expenses**
- Total dollar amount of sales commissions
- Total dollar amount of finder's fees

**Item 16: Use of Proceeds**
- Gross proceeds used for payments to related persons
- Estimated net offering proceeds
- Clarification of use (payments for salaries, etc.)

### 4.3 Filing Timeline

- **Initial filing:** Within **15 calendar days** after the **first sale** of securities
- A "sale" occurs when an investor's funds are received and accepted by the issuer
- Can file before first sale (and many advisors recommend doing so)
- **No filing fee** -- EDGAR filing is free

### 4.4 Amendment Requirements

**Annual amendment:** Required if the offering continues beyond 12 months. Due on or before the one-year anniversary of the most recent filing or amendment.

**Material change amendments:** File "as soon as practicable" when:
- The offering has ended
- There is a change in the exemption claimed
- A change in issuer name or legal status
- A change in industry group
- Changes to related persons (new officers, directors)
- Any other material change

**Changes that do NOT require immediate amendment:**
- Changes to addresses of related persons
- Changes in issuer size (revenue/assets)
- Changes to minimum investment amount
- Changes in states of solicitation

## 5. Accredited Investor Verification

### 5.1 Legal Requirements Under 506(c)

Rule 506(c) imposes a **mandatory verification obligation** -- the issuer must take "reasonable steps to verify" that each purchaser is an accredited investor. This is the key distinction from 506(b), where self-certification is generally sufficient.

The SEC uses a **principles-based approach** -- what constitutes "reasonable steps" depends on the facts and circumstances of each transaction, including:
- The nature of the purchaser (individual vs. entity)
- The amount and type of information the issuer has about the purchaser
- The nature of the offering (minimum investment amounts, how purchasers were solicited)

### 5.2 Self-Certification Rules for Investments $200K+ (March 2025 Game-Changer)

On **March 12, 2025**, the SEC Division of Corporation Finance issued a landmark no-action letter that dramatically simplified verification:

**For natural persons investing $200,000+:**
The issuer can rely on self-certification if:
1. The minimum investment amount is at least **$200,000**
2. The issuer obtains a **written representation** from the purchaser that:
   - They are an accredited investor
   - The investment is **not financed** by a third party for the purpose of making the investment
3. The issuer has **no information** suggesting the representation is false

**For entities investing $1,000,000+:**
The same simplified approach applies with a $1,000,000 minimum.

**Why this matters for PleoChrome:** Given that a $10-18M gemstone offering will likely have minimum investments well above $200,000, PleoChrome may be able to rely on self-certification for most or all investors, dramatically reducing verification friction and cost.

## 6. Transfer Agent Requirements

### 6.1 Do You Need One? Why?

**Yes, you need a transfer agent for a tokenized securities offering.** Here is why:

A transfer agent is the official recordkeeper of who owns a company's securities. For tokenized securities, the transfer agent:
- Maintains the **official shareholder registry** (cap table)
- Processes **transfers and ownership changes**
- Ensures compliance with **transfer restrictions** (Rule 144 holding periods, investor eligibility)
- Handles **corporate actions** (distributions, voting, redemptions)
- Prevents unauthorized transfers of restricted securities
- Provides **regulatory accountability** (SEC-registered entity with fiduciary duties)

For a tokenized offering specifically, the transfer agent ensures that blockchain records align with legal ownership records and that smart contract transfers comply with securities law restrictions.

### 6.2 SEC Registration Requirements

- It provides institutional credibility
- Ensures proper compliance controls
- Many investors and broker-dealers require it
- The SEC is actively pursuing **transfer agent reform** (targeted for early 2026 rulemaking) that may impose new requirements

## 7. Broker-Dealer Requirements

### 7.1 When Do You Need a Broker-Dealer?

A broker-dealer (BD) is required when **anyone receives transaction-based compensation** (commissions) for soliciting or selling securities. Key scenarios:

**You NEED a BD if:**
- You pay finders, salespeople, or intermediaries commissions based on sales
- You list on a platform that receives transaction-based fees
- You use agents who actively solicit investors for compensation
- The tokenization platform facilitates secondary trading

**You may NOT need a BD if:**
- All sales are made directly by issuer employees (issuer exemption -- Section 3(a)(4) of the Exchange Act)
- No transaction-based compensation is paid to anyone
- The offering is sold entirely through direct marketing without intermediaries
- Issuer personnel who sell receive only fixed salary (not commissions tied to sales)

**Important:** A person who sells securities exempt from registration under Regulation D must still register as a broker-dealer if they are "in the business of" effecting securities transactions. The exemption from Securities Act registration does NOT exempt from broker-dealer registration.

### 7.2 FINRA Registration

If a BD is needed:
- Must register with the **SEC** (Form BD)
- Must become a member of **FINRA** (Financial Industry Regulatory Authority)
- Must register in each state where they conduct business
- Must comply with FINRA rules including:
  - **Suitability** (now "Reg BI" -- best interest) obligations
  - **Anti-money laundering** programs
  - **Net capital** requirements
  - **Books and records** requirements
  - **Filing of offering documents** with FINRA within 15 days of first sale (private placements)

### 7.3 Exemptions That Might Apply

**A. Issuer Exemption (Section 3(a)(4) of the Exchange Act)**
- Company employees can sell their own company's securities without BD registration
- Must be bona fide employees, not independent contractors
- Cannot receive commission-based compensation specifically tied to sales
- Limited to the issuer's own securities

**B. M&A Broker Exemption (2023)**
- Only applies to M&A transactions, not capital raises
- Not applicable to PleoChrome's offering

**C. Funding Portal (Reg CF only)**
- Only applies to Regulation Crowdfunding offerings
- Not applicable to Reg D

**D. Using an existing registered BD**
- Most practical approach: engage a registered BD firm to handle distribution
- The BD takes responsibility for compliance and investor suitability

### 7.4 Cost of Engaging a Broker-Dealer

| Model | Cost |
|-------|------|
| **Success-fee BD (selling agent)** | 1-10% of capital raised (typically 5-8% for alternative assets) |
| **Placement agent (institutional)** | 2-5% of capital raised + potential warrants/equity |
| **Platform BD (Securitize, tZERO)** | Platform fees + 1-3% transaction fees |
| **Annual BD subscription** | $10,000 - $50,000/year (for platform access) |

**For PleoChrome's $10-18M offering:**
- If using a selling agent at 5%: **$500,000 - $900,000** in commissions
- If using a platform BD: **$50,000 - $200,000** in total fees
- If selling directly (issuer exemption): **$0** in BD fees, but limited to employee-only sales

2. Supplement with a **placement agent** for investor introductions (especially for $1M+ checks)

## 8. Ongoing Compliance

### 8.1 Annual Filing Requirements

**Form D Annual Amendment:**
- Required if the offering continues beyond 12 months
- File on or before the one-year anniversary of the most recent filing/amendment
- If you file an amendment at any point, the clock resets to one year from that amendment

**State Blue Sky Renewals:**
- Many states require annual renewal of notice filings
- Fees generally the same as initial filing
- Track state-specific deadlines carefully

**FINRA (if BD involved):**
- Ongoing reporting obligations through FINRA gateway
- Annual compliance attestations

### 8.2 Investor Communications

While Reg D has minimal mandatory reporting, **your operating agreement should specify:**
- **Quarterly or annual financial reports** to investors
- **Distribution notices** when proceeds are distributed
- **Material event notifications** (significant changes to the asset, custody arrangements, etc.)
- **Tax documents** (K-1s or 1099s) annually
- **Annual meeting** or written consent for major decisions

**Best practice for PleoChrome:** Provide quarterly updates on:
- Gemstone valuation and market conditions
- Custody and insurance status
- Any appraisal updates
- Fund financial statements
- Regulatory developments affecting the offering

### 8.3 Bad Actor Checks

**Rule 506(d) -- "Bad Actor" Disqualification:**

An offering is disqualified from Rule 506 if any "covered person" has had a "disqualifying event."

**Covered Persons include:**
- The issuer (PleoChrome/SPV entity)
- Any beneficial owner of 20%+ voting equity
- Directors, officers, general partners, managing members
- Promoters (at time of sale)
- Any person compensated for soliciting investors (and their directors, officers, GP)
- Investment managers and their directors/officers (for pooled investment funds)

**Disqualifying Events include:**
- Criminal convictions (securities-related) within 10 years (5 years for issuer)
- Court injunctions/restraining orders in securities cases within 5 years
- Final orders from securities, banking, or insurance regulators within 10 years
- SEC disciplinary orders within 10 years
- SEC cease-and-desist orders involving scienter-based violations within 10 years
- SEC stop orders or refusal orders within 5 years
- USPS false representation orders within 5 years

**"Reasonable Care" Exception:**
If you did not know and could not have known (with reasonable care) about a disqualifying event, the offering is not disqualified. However, you must conduct a **factual inquiry** -- you cannot simply "not ask."

**Practical steps:**
1. Require each covered person to complete a bad actor questionnaire
2. Conduct background checks (CrowdCheck provides this as a service)
3. Document the inquiry process
4. Repeat checks when new covered persons are added

### 8.4 Transfer Restrictions (Rule 144 Holding Periods)

Securities sold under Reg D are **restricted securities** and cannot be freely resold. Investors must understand:

**Rule 144 Holding Period:**
- **Non-reporting issuer** (typical for Reg D SPV): **One year** holding period
- **SEC-reporting company:** Six months

**After the holding period, additional conditions for resale:**
- **Current public information** must be available (for reporting companies)
- **Volume limitations** (for affiliates only): Greater of 1% of outstanding shares or average weekly trading volume
- **Manner of sale** requirements (for affiliates): Ordinary brokerage transactions
- **Filing requirement** (for affiliates): File Form 144 with SEC if selling 5,000+ shares or $50,000+

**For tokenized securities specifically:**
- Smart contracts should enforce transfer restrictions programmatically
- Whitelist-based transfer controls ensure only eligible purchasers can receive tokens
- Transfer agent must verify compliance before processing any transfer
- Secondary market trading (if available) is limited to verified accredited investors during the holding period

**Practical impact for PleoChrome:**
- Investors cannot sell their tokens for at least **one year** (since PleoChrome's SPV will not be an SEC-reporting company)
- After one year, resales are still restricted -- must comply with Rule 144 conditions
- Smart contracts and transfer agent controls should enforce these restrictions automatically
- A secondary market on platforms like tZERO could facilitate compliant resales after the holding period

### 8.5 What Happens If You Screw Up

**Failure to file Form D:**
- Technically, Form D filing is not a condition of the Reg D exemption itself
- However, the SEC can seek an injunction prohibiting future reliance on Reg D exemptions
- States may impose penalties, fines, or deny the exemption for failure to file blue sky notices
- The SEC has proposed (but not yet adopted) rules that would make Form D filing a condition of the exemption

**Selling to non-accredited investors:**
- Loss of the 506(c) exemption entirely
- The offering becomes an unregistered sale of securities -- a violation of Section 5 of the Securities Act
- Investors have a **right of rescission** (can demand their money back + interest)
- SEC enforcement action (fines, injunctions, potential criminal referral)
- State enforcement actions
- Private lawsuits by investors

**Inadequate verification:**
- Even if all investors are in fact accredited, failure to take "reasonable steps to verify" violates Rule 506(c)
- Loss of exemption, rescission rights, enforcement risk

**Anti-fraud violations (misleading PPM or marketing):**
- Section 10(b) and Rule 10b-5 liability (SEC enforcement + private lawsuits)
- Section 17(a) of the Securities Act liability
- State anti-fraud enforcement
- Personal liability for officers and directors
- Potential criminal prosecution for willful violations

**Bad actor disqualification:**
- If a disqualifying event is discovered and the offering did not qualify for the "reasonable care" exception, the entire offering loses its exemption
- All sales become unregistered sales -- rescission rights for all investors

The SEC's Division of Trading and Markets issued a no-action letter to the **Depository Trust Company (DTC)** enabling a **three-year pilot program** for tokenizing DTC-custodied assets.

**Key details:**
- DTC can tokenize security entitlements to eligible securities on supported blockchains
- **Eligible securities** (pilot phase): Russell 1000 Index stocks, U.S. Treasury securities, and ETFs tracking S&P 500/Nasdaq-100

- **Expected launch:** Second half of 2026

**Impact on PleoChrome:** While PleoChrome's gemstone tokens are not DTC-eligible securities, this letter is enormously significant because:
1. It legitimizes tokenization at the highest level of US securities infrastructure
2. It establishes that blockchain-based records can be compatible with existing securities law
3. It signals regulatory acceptance of the technology PleoChrome is using
4. It may eventually lead to broader tokenization programs that could include alternative assets

### 10.3 SEC Statement on Tokenized Securities (January 28, 2026)

Staff from three SEC divisions (Corporation Finance, Investment Management, Trading and Markets) jointly issued a landmark **Statement on Tokenized Securities**.

**Core principle:** "Changing the format of a security (to a token or other crypto asset) does not change whether, or how, the federal securities laws apply." Tokenization alters the "plumbing, not the regulatory perimeter."

**Two categories of tokenized securities defined:**

**A. Issuer-Sponsored Tokenized Securities:**
- Created by or on behalf of the issuer
- Token transactions reflected in official ownership records
- Holders have direct rights against the issuer
- Can use blockchain as the master securityholder file, OR use tokens within traditional offchain transfer mechanics
- Subject to standard Securities Act registration (or exemption), reporting, and corporate governance

**B. Third-Party-Sponsored Tokenized Securities:**
- Created by third parties unaffiliated with the issuer
- Two sub-types:
  - **Custodial:** Third parties hold securities and create tokenized entitlements -- similar to ETFs
  - **Synthetic:** New securities providing economic exposure without ownership rights -- may be classified as security-based swaps with retail sale restrictions

**Impact on PleoChrome:** PleoChrome's model is **issuer-sponsored tokenization** (PleoChrome/SPV issues tokens representing ownership in the gemstone barrel). This is the straightforward path: standard securities laws apply, Reg D 506(c) exemption works as expected, and the token is simply the ownership format. The SEC's statement confirms this approach is well-understood and accepted.

- Divides crypto assets into three categories: **digital commodities**, **investment contract assets**, and **permitted payment stablecoins**
- Defines CFTC jurisdiction over digital commodities
- Defines SEC jurisdiction over investment contract assets
- Creates regulatory framework for stablecoins

**Current bottleneck:** Senate Banking Committee markup delayed due to disputes over stablecoin yield and non-bank interest payments.

**Impact on PleoChrome:** The CLARITY Act would provide additional regulatory certainty for the digital asset ecosystem. PleoChrome's tokens would likely fall under "investment contract assets" (SEC jurisdiction), confirming the Reg D approach. The Act's passage would further legitimize tokenized securities.

### 10.7 SEC Transfer Agent Reform (Expected 2026)

The SEC has prioritized **transfer agent reform** as a target for rulemaking in early 2026. Key goals:
- Create greater equivalence between directly registered shares and beneficial ownership forms
- Address the responsibilities and capabilities of transfer agents in alternative operating models (including blockchain-based recordkeeping)
- Level the playing field between traditional and digital transfer agents

**Impact on PleoChrome:** New rules may impose additional requirements on digital transfer agents like Securitize, Vertalo, etc. Monitor this rulemaking carefully and ensure your transfer agent is prepared to comply with any new requirements.
