## Week 1

### Saturday — "The banker who couldn't stop building"

When all is said and done, I spend my weeks helping business owners navigate SBA lending. I love that work. It's what I know. But on nights and weekends, I've been deep in something completely different.

I've been researching how high-value physical assets — gemstones, real estate, mineral rights — get monetized. Not stocks. Not crypto. Real things sitting in vaults and safety deposit boxes that represent enormous value but have zero liquidity.

Here's the number that got me: the global gemstone market is worth $34 billion a year. Thirty-four billion. And there's no exchange for it. No ticker symbol. No secondary market. If you own a $10 million emerald lot and you need capital, your options are basically "find a private buyer" or "wait."

That gap between what an asset is worth and what an owner can do with that value — that's the problem I've been pulling apart. Five different value creation models. Tokenization. Fractional securities. Debt instruments. Broker sale. Barter exchange.

I wrote a deep analysis on the tokenization side. A researcher at Macquarie University published data showing that most tokenized real-world assets trade about once per year after issuance. Once. Per. Year. BlackRock's tokenized treasury fund has $2 billion in assets and 85 holders.

The infrastructure to create tokens is years ahead of the infrastructure to trade them. That's the real story nobody talks about.

### Sunday — "Five questions I'd ask before tokenizing anything"

I come from lending. When someone walks into my office wanting a loan, I ask questions before I run numbers. What's the collateral? What's the exit? Who's the borrower? What's the realistic timeline?

Turns out the same discipline applies to tokenization. Maybe even more so, because the marketing around tokenized assets makes it sound like you mint a token and liquidity appears. It does not.

Five questions I'd want answered before putting a dollar into structuring a tokenized offering:

1. Who is the buyer? If you can't describe your investor in one sentence, a token won't find them either.

2. What's the realistic holding period? If your investors are going to hold for two years, maybe you don't need a secondary market. Maybe a debt instrument works better.

3. What does verification cost? Under Rule 506(c), every investor has to be verified as accredited. The SEC simplified this in March 2025 for offerings with $200K+ minimums, but below that threshold, you're collecting tax returns.

4. Can the asset be priced continuously? Stocks have tickers. A one-of-one 47-carat Paraiba tourmaline does not. That opacity affects everything downstream.

5. What's your exit plan for investors? "Secondary market" is easy to say. Building one is a completely different conversation.

None of these have wrong answers. They have honest answers. And the honest answers should drive which model you choose — not which platform you talked to first.

## Week 2

### Saturday — "What I learned researching something outside my lane"

I've spent 20+ years in banking. SBA lending. Credit analysis. Capital markets from the institutional side. I know that world.

So when I started researching real-world asset tokenization about a year ago, I did what any good credit guy would do: I pulled the data and looked for the gaps between what people promise and what actually happens.

The research is fascinating. And honestly, a little sobering.

The RWA tokenization market grew from $5 billion to $36 billion in three years. That sounds incredible until you look at where the money went. Private credit and U.S. Treasuries make up 85% of it. These are institutional yield products held to maturity. Nobody's actively trading them.

Real estate tokenization? $300 million total. Fine art? $100 million. Gemstones? Basically zero in tracked markets.

The asset classes that would benefit most from fractional ownership and global accessibility represent less than 3% of the total market. That's not a failure. It's a sequencing problem. The infrastructure started with the easy stuff (government debt) and hasn't reached the hard stuff yet (unique physical assets with subjective valuations).

When all is said and done, the technology works. The legal frameworks exist. What's missing is the market infrastructure — the custody solutions, the compliance rails, the trading venues — that makes these assets actually tradable after issuance.

That's the part I find interesting. Not the blockchain part. The market structure part. Because that's what I've spent my career thinking about, just in a different context.

### Sunday — "The SEC actually did something useful"

I know. I know. Bankers complaining about regulators is basically a sport at this point. But credit where it's due.

In March 2025, the SEC issued guidance that simplified how issuers verify accredited investors under Rule 506(c). If your minimum investment is $200,000 or more, a written self-certification from the investor is now sufficient. No more collecting tax returns. No more third-party verification letters at $250-$500 a pop.

For anyone structuring a private placement for alternative assets, this is a meaningful cost reduction and a major friction reduction. Some issuers avoided 506(c) entirely because the verification burden pushed investors away during onboarding. Now, for higher-minimum offerings, that friction is basically gone.

Then in January 2026, the SEC issued a joint statement from three divisions clarifying that tokenized securities are still securities. Sounds obvious. But the practical implication is important: if you're issuing LLC membership interests and representing them as tokens, you follow the same rules as any other private placement. No new exemption needed. No special framework. The rules didn't change. The guidance on how to follow them got clearer.

For the builder types out there working on alternative asset offerings: the regulatory environment is more favorable right now than it was 18 months ago. Not because the rules changed, but because the instructions got better.

## Week 3

### Saturday — "I built a CRM in my garage"

Yes, I am that guy. The one who builds things at 5 AM before the rest of the house wakes up.

When I started mapping the workflow for taking a physical asset from a vault to an investment product, I counted 92 discrete tasks across 35 stages and 6 phases. KYC on the holder. GIA certification. Three independent appraisals. Custody transfer. SPV formation. PPM drafting. Investor verification. Compliance monitoring. Distribution reporting.

Salesforce doesn't have a field for "chain of custody documentation." HubSpot doesn't track UCC-1 filing status. So I built it.

It's called Powerhouse. Every task has an owner, a status, a due date, and an audit trail. Every document is version-controlled. Every phase advancement is gated by completion criteria. The team logs in through Google OAuth and works through the stages on their phone or desktop.

Is it overkill for a three-person operation? Probably. But here's what 20 years in banking taught me: if your operations are complex enough to require multiple specialists, multiple compliance frameworks, and multiple phases, you need purpose-built workflow tooling. General-purpose tools create general-purpose outcomes.

The lesson applies way beyond what I'm building. Whatever your business is, if the process has more than 10 steps and involves more than 2 people, a spreadsheet is not a system. It's a liability waiting to happen.

### Sunday — "There are four ways to test if anyone cares before you spend $100K"

This is the credit guy in me talking. Before you underwrite a deal, you qualify it. Before you fund it, you stress test it. The same logic applies to launching an alternative asset offering.

Legal fees for a Reg D 506(c) private placement run $80,000 to $150,000. That's before a single investor writes a check. Appraisals, custody, broker-dealer engagement, marketing — you're looking at six figures committed to find out if the market wants what you're building.

Or you could test first.

Option 1: Publish research. Build a landing page. Run some LinkedIn ads. Track who shows up. Cost: $5K-$15K. If nobody reads your content, nobody is going to invest in your offering.

Option 2: Reg CF Testing the Waters. The SEC actually allows you to formally gauge interest before filing. You collect non-binding indications of interest with dollar amounts. Real data. Not guesses. Cost: $10K-$25K.

Option 3: Talk to 20 people who match your investor profile. Not a pitch. A conversation. "What would make you allocate here? What would make you pass?" The body language when someone says "I'd be interested at $100K minimums" tells you more than a form submission ever will.

The March 2025 SEC guidance makes all of this more practical. Self-certification at $200K+ minimums means your eventual 506(c) offering has lower friction and lower cost.

Test before you build. Qualify before you underwrite. Same discipline, different asset class.

## Week 4

### Saturday — "Tokenization, fractional, debt — they're not competing ideas"

People keep asking me which value creation model is "the best one." The honest answer is that the question misses the point.

Think about it like lending. A business doesn't just need one product. They might need an SBA 7(a) for acquisition, a line of credit for working capital, and equipment financing for the build-out. Three different structures, one business, layered together.

Same concept with high-value assets. Consider a $10 million gemstone collection:

You could take a collateralized loan against it (UCC Article 9, asset-backed debt). That funds the legal costs without selling anything.

Then you structure fractional securities (Reg D 506(c) LLC units) to bring in investor capital at lower minimums.

Those LLC units could eventually be tokenized for programmable compliance, automated distributions, and a path to secondary trading.

Three models. One asset. Sequenced based on where the asset is in its lifecycle and what the market is ready for.

The right answer isn't which model. It's which model first, and what comes next.

That's what I'm building. Not instead of what I do during the week. Because of what I've learned doing it.
